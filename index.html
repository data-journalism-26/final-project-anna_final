Price Convergence · Central & Eastern Europe

The Cost of
Catching Up

Eastern Europe is converging with Western Europe in price levels — but the relationship between prices, incomes, and productivity remains profoundly uneven. A decade of data tells a complex story of progress, shocks, and persistent gaps.

Analysis based on Eurostat data  ·  Price Level Index, GDP per capita, Median Income & HICP

A single market, a shared currency for some, and three decades of integration: yet Europe's economic map still splits sharply along a diagonal running roughly from the Baltic to the Adriatic. On one side lie the high-price, high-income economies of Northwestern Europe. On the other, the Central and Eastern European states that joined the EU in 2004 and after, still chasing a standard of living their western neighbours take for granted.

Price Level Index data from Eurostat — measuring what a basket of goods and services actually costs relative to the EU average of 100 — captures this divide with clinical precision. What follows is a decade-long portrait of convergence that is real, but fragile.

01 · 2015 Baseline

A continent divided at the starting line

In 2015, the price divide across Europe was stark. Switzerland topped the index at 170 — nearly three-and-a-half times the level of North Macedonia at 48. The EU-28 average stood at 100, but virtually every Central and Eastern European member state sat well below it.

Bulgaria and Romania hovered near 50 — meaning a basket of goods costing €100 in the EU on average cost just €50 there. Poland and Hungary sat in the 57–61 range. Even the more developed Czechia reached only 67, while Germany anchored Northwestern Europe at 107 and France and the Netherlands at 112–114.

02 · Convergence Trend 2015–2024

A decade of rising prices in the East — but Northwest holds steady

Over the following nine years, CEE EU member states recorded consistent increases in their price levels. The most dramatic case is Czechia: from 66.8 in 2015 to a peak of 91.7 by 2023, closing to within 10 points of the EU average for the first time. Poland rose from 57.6 to 72.3. Croatia climbed from 67.8 to 76.6.

The Baltic states — Estonia, Latvia, Lithuania — recorded particularly sharp post-2021 rises, reflecting their tight integration with EU energy markets and high exposure to supply-chain shocks.

Northwestern Europe, by contrast, showed remarkable stability. Germany, France, Austria, the Netherlands, and Belgium all remained within a narrow 107–117 band throughout the decade, barely moving despite external shocks.

Convergence is real — but asymmetric. Prices in Eastern Europe are rising faster than wages, squeezing households even as the statistical gap with the West narrows.

03 · Income vs. Output

GDP grows — but median incomes tell a different story

By 2024, a scatter of GDP per capita (in Purchasing Power Standards) against median household income reveals a Europe that remains sharply segmented. Luxembourg's GDP of 245 and median income of €37,781 stand in a class of their own. Norway, Switzerland, and Austria cluster in a high-income, high-output zone.

CEE member states — Bulgaria, Hungary, Romania, Slovakia — record GDP indices of 66–91, but their median incomes cluster between €11,000 and €17,000 per year. The EU average for median income is €21,245, a threshold most CEE countries remain well below.

Ireland is the most striking outlier: GDP per capita at 221 — inflated by multinational corporate accounting — against a median income of €23,288, closer to France or Germany. The GDP figure flatters the Irish economy; the income figure is more honest about lived standards.

The takeaway: GDP convergence overstates living-standard convergence. In CEE economies, growth is real — but much of the gain flows to capital, not wages.

04 · The Inflation Shock

2022: The East takes the harder hit

The inflation shock of 2021–2023 disrupted the convergence trajectory across Europe, but its impact was far from uniform. Countries with higher 2022 HICP inflation — a measure of consumer price change — also tended to see sharper rises in their Price Level Index between 2021 and 2023.

The Baltic states suffered the most: Estonia recorded HICP inflation of 19.4% in 2022, Lithuania 18.9%, Latvia 17.2%. All three saw their PLI rise by 7.5–11 points in two years. Hungary posted 15.3% inflation with a 6-point PLI rise. Poland, with 13.2%, gained 7.7 points.

Northwestern economies fared better. Switzerland's HICP of 2.7% barely moved its PLI. France at 5.9% saw its PLI fall slightly. Germany at 8.7% rose by less than 2 points. The insulation reflects deeper monetary buffers, energy price controls, and more diversified supply chains.

Norway is a notable outlier — its PLI fell sharply by 14 points despite modest inflation, likely reflecting kroner appreciation effects in the index calculation.

The Architecture of a Divide

To understand why prices in Eastern Europe are lower — and why that gap is slowly closing — requires stepping back from the statistics into the structural realities of European economic geography. When Poland, Hungary, the Czech Republic, Slovakia, Slovenia, Estonia, Latvia, and Lithuania joined the European Union in 2004, they entered a single market with a single set of rules but profoundly different cost structures. Wages were a fraction of Western European levels. Land, services, and energy were cheaper. Productivity, measured by GDP per hour worked, lagged significantly.

This is the Balassa-Samuelson effect in action: economies with lower productivity in tradeable sectors — manufacturing, agriculture, export services — tend to have lower price levels across the board, because wages in non-tradeable sectors (retail, housing, haircuts) track the wage level set in the tradeable sector. As productivity rises and wages increase, prices throughout the economy follow. This is not a failure — it is the mechanism of convergence working as intended.

The data from 2015 to 2024 confirms this story, but with important caveats. The countries converging fastest are not always those where households feel best off.

Czechia: The Fastest Convergence Story

No country in the dataset better illustrates the speed — and the tensions — of convergence than Czechia. Starting from a PLI of 66.8 in 2015, already the highest among the Visegrád group, it surged to 91.7 by 2023 before pulling back slightly to 88.5 in 2024. In less than a decade, Czech price levels nearly reached the EU average. This reflects a genuinely high-performing economy: strong export manufacturing, a tight labour market, and rising wages that have fed through into service prices and housing costs.

But convergence in prices has not been matched by convergence in incomes. Czech median income in 2024 stands at €16,972 — higher than Bulgaria or Romania, but still well below the EU average of €21,245 and less than half the Austrian figure of €29,758. Czech households are increasingly paying Western European prices on Central European wages. That compression is the central tension of price convergence.

"Catching up in prices is not the same as catching up in living standards. The former can happen within a decade. The latter takes a generation."

The Baltic Exception

Estonia, Latvia, and Lithuania occupy a particular position in this story. All three adopted the euro relatively early — Estonia in 2011, Latvia in 2014, Lithuania in 2015 — and have pursued tight fiscal and monetary policies aligned with Eurozone norms. Their integration into Nordic supply chains, German manufacturing networks, and Scandinavian retail markets has accelerated price transmission in both directions.

The result: by 2024, Estonia's PLI of 100.2 has essentially converged with the EU average — a remarkable achievement for a country at 77.5 in 2015. Latvia and Lithuania, at 82.7 and 81.7 respectively, are not far behind. Yet the 2022 inflation crisis exposed the vulnerability of this position. With HICP inflation above 17–19%, households faced a brutal squeeze: prices surging at Western European rates, wages still rooted in the Baltic reality. The PLI rose 8–11 points in two years, compressing real purchasing power sharply.

Northwestern Europe: The Stability Premium

What is perhaps most striking about the Northwestern European data is its sheer stability. Germany's PLI moved within a 105–109 range across the entire decade. France barely shifted from 112 to 111. The Netherlands, Belgium, and Austria all exhibit similarly flat trajectories. For economies this large and this integrated, such stability in relative price levels reflects deep structural anchors: diversified energy sourcing, strong trade union wage-setting, mature service sectors, and credible central bank frameworks.

The inflation shock of 2022 was absorbed with far less PLI disruption than in the East. France's PLI actually declined slightly, suggesting that French domestic prices rose more slowly than the EU average — an effect of government energy price caps and regulated tariffs. Germany, with its heavier exposure to gas prices, saw a modest 2-point rise. Switzerland, with HICP inflation of just 2.7%, barely moved at all.

The GDP Illusion

The scatter plot of GDP per capita against median income reveals something important about what economic statistics can and cannot tell us. Ireland's GDP per capita of 221 — more than twice the EU average — is one of the most distorted figures in European statistics, inflated by the profits of multinational corporations domiciled there for tax purposes. Strip that out, and Irish living standards, while genuinely high, are closer to Austria or Denmark than to Luxembourg.

For CEE economies, the distortion runs in the opposite direction. Romania's GDP of 77 and median income of €13,023 suggests a country performing broadly in line with its productivity level. But Bulgaria's GDP of 66 against a median income of €13,079 — actually fractionally above Romania despite lower GDP — hints at different distributional patterns within each economy. Croatia, with GDP at 78 and median income of €16,277, punches above its GDP weight on household income, suggesting a more equal distribution of recent growth.

What Convergence Actually Means

The decade from 2015 to 2024 produced genuine convergence in price levels between Central and Eastern Europe and Northwestern Europe. Bulgaria moved from 49.7 to 60.5. Romania from 55.1 to 64.1. Poland from 57.6 to 72.3. These are real, structural shifts — not statistical noise.

But the inflation shock of 2021–2023 accelerated price convergence in ways that outpaced income convergence. For households in Warsaw, Bucharest, or Vilnius, the result was a cost-of-living squeeze that felt sharper than it did in Berlin or Amsterdam. Prices converged upward faster than wages could follow.

The deepest lesson from this data is that convergence is not a single process — it is several overlapping ones, moving at different speeds. Price convergence is measurable and relatively fast. Income convergence is slower, shaped by wage bargaining, education, and institutional quality. Productivity convergence — the underlying driver of both — is slower still, measured in decades and conditioned on investment, infrastructure, and human capital.

Europe's eastern economies have made extraordinary progress since accession. But the cost of catching up — paid in squeezed household budgets, in rising rents, in the emigration of workers seeking wages that match the prices at home — remains unevenly distributed. The gap is narrowing. The journey is not over.
