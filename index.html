Are prices in Central and Eastern Europe catching up with the West?

Europe is highly diverse — high-income countries tend to have high price levels, but that alone tells us little about actual living standards. Price Level Index data from Eurostat, measuring the cost of a basket of goods and services relative to the EU average of 100, helps make this distinction clearer.

Europe is far from economically uniform. Countries in Northwestern Europe combine high incomes with high prices, while Central and Eastern European countries sit at the other end — lower wages, lower costs, lower price levels. But high prices do not automatically mean a better quality of life. A country can be expensive precisely because it is wealthy, or because its costs are structurally high regardless of what people actually earn.

To look beyond GDP and cut closer to lived experience, it helps to use the Price Level Index (PLI) published by Eurostat. It measures what a basket of goods and services actually costs in a given country relative to the EU average of 100. A PLI of 50 means things are roughly half the EU average price; a PLI of 150 means they are half again more expensive. The decade from 2015 to 2024 shows that CEE countries are steadily converging upward — but not at the same pace across all dimensions.

A continent divided by a line

Looking at the 2015 map, the split between higher- and lower-cost countries is immediately visible. Switzerland sits at the top with a PLI of 170 — nearly 3.5 times the level of North Macedonia at 48. That means the same basket of goods cost 3.5 times more in Switzerland than in North Macedonia.

Bulgaria and Romania were close to 50, Poland at 57.6, Hungary at 61.4. Even Czechia, the most developed among the Visegrád group, reached only 66.8. Western Europe clustered firmly above 100: Germany at 107, France and the Netherlands around 112–114.

The line dividing Europe was not just statistical — it reflected genuinely different cost structures: lower wages, cheaper services, lower productivity.

Ten years of rising prices in the East

Between 2015 and 2024, all CEE EU member states recorded a steady rise in their Price Level Index. The most dramatic case is Czechia, which went from 66.8 in 2015 to 91.7 in 2023 — coming within 10 points of the EU average for the first time. Poland rose from 57.6 to 72.3. Croatia from 67.8 to 76.6.

The Baltic states — Estonia, Latvia, Lithuania — saw particularly sharp increases after 2021. Estonia’s PLI crossed 100 by 2024, essentially reaching the EU average. Northwestern European countries, meanwhile, barely moved: Germany stayed in a 105–109 band, France around 112–113, the Netherlands around 114–115.

GDP grows, but median incomes lag behind

The 2024 scatter plot comparing GDP per capita with median household income shows that the two do not always move together. The CEE cluster sits in the lower-left: GDP indices of 66–91, median incomes mostly between €11,000 and €17,000. The EU median is €21,245 — a threshold most CEE countries still fall short of.

Luxembourg stands apart entirely, with a GDP index of 245 and median income of €37,781. Ireland is the most notable outlier: a GDP of 221, heavily inflated by multinationals booking profits there, against a median income of €23,288 — closer to France or Germany in practical terms.

The CEE cluster is less tightly correlated — countries at similar GDP levels show more varied income outcomes, which hints at different distributional dynamics within those economies.

The inflation shock hit the East harder

The inflation crisis of 2021–2023 disrupted the convergence trend. Countries that were already converging upward in price levels were hit by additional inflationary pressure — and CEE countries took the brunt of it.

Estonia recorded HICP inflation of 19.4% in 2022, Lithuania 18.9%, Latvia 17.2%. All three saw their PLI jump by 7.5–11 points in just two years. Hungary posted 15.3% inflation, Poland 13.2%, Czechia 14.8%. In each case, prices converged upward faster than wages could follow.

Northwestern economies were largely insulated. Switzerland saw just 2.7% HICP inflation and barely moved. France’s PLI actually declined slightly. Germany rose by less than 2 points.

Europe has always been economically diverse, but the scale of that diversity is easy to underestimate. When Central and Eastern European countries joined the EU, they brought cost structures shaped by decades of different economic development. Wages were a fraction of Western levels, services were cheaper, and so were energy and housing. Price convergence — the gradual rise of CEE price levels toward the EU average — was expected to follow as productivity grew and wages rose.

The data confirms this is happening. But it also shows that convergence is not a single, smooth process. Prices can rise faster than wages. GDP can grow faster than household incomes. And external shocks — like the inflation surge of 2021–2023 — can accelerate price convergence in ways that actually worsen living standards rather than improve them.

Convergence, and its costs

Price convergence between Central and Eastern Europe and Northwestern Europe is real. Bulgaria, Romania, Poland — all have moved meaningfully closer to the EU average over the past decade. But convergence in prices does not automatically mean convergence in living standards. The inflation shock of 2021–2023 made this especially clear: the countries converging most rapidly were hit hardest, and the compression of real purchasing power will take years to unwind.

The gap between East and West in Europe is narrowing — but prices, incomes, and productivity are closing at different speeds. For households in Warsaw, Prague, or Vilnius, the statistical convergence is real. The lived experience of it is considerably more complicated.
